# Anti-Involution

Every year, more than ten million university students graduate, and many people choose to deliver food just to make a living. If we still force children to follow the traditional exam-oriented path, they may end up facing the predicament of “graduation equals unemployment.” I don’t believe the government can completely solve involution in the short term, because policy adjustments are hard to reverse the situation quickly. Only a more fully market-oriented system can ease this problem.

When it comes to education, the realistic choices seem clear: families with financial resources can send children to international schools as early as elementary school; families without such conditions need to avoid the trap of involution early—reducing endless drill-and-practice from primary school and instead emphasizing reading ability, physical exercise, curiosity, and resilience.

However, the biggest obstacle to choosing international schools is often not money, but parents’ beliefs. Parents who grew up in a collectivist culture may find it difficult to adapt when their children shift toward a more market-oriented and individualized educational environment.

> My favorite writer, Ernest Hemingway, once said: “A man can be destroyed but not defeated.”

Perhaps the greatest tragedy in life is seeing clearly that the road ahead is a pit—yet still choosing to jump in.
