# Learning Environment

To help children grow and learn better, the environment plays a crucial role. Compared with the external environment, the family atmosphere has a deeper influence. Learning through subtle daily exposure is far more effective than rote memorization. Today I want to share how our family creates a learning environment.

Parents are a child’s earliest role models. When a parent loves learning, the child naturally imitates what the adult does. As parents, we should first be clear about the learning priorities at the current stage. For example, my son is in elementary school. At this stage the core is Chinese, math, and English, combined with exercise and interests. If we had to rank them, in our family we put exercise first, interests second, and academics after that.

Sports and interests are areas children can naturally engage in: sports release energy and promote physical development, but many parents keep children at home and lack outdoor activity, which is very unfavorable for growth. Interests such as singing, drawing, and dancing usually require little pushing—children often do them proactively. As long as these two are done well, we can shift the focus to “the minimum necessary knowledge,” namely Chinese, math, and English.

The key is to make learning enjoyable.

Chinese and English are both language subjects, and the focus is communication and expression. Our family emphasizes extensive reading. Every day, the time spent reading is at least twice the time spent on homework. Even if homework is not finished, we still prioritize reading. English learning is similar: besides reading, we create context through cartoons, listening practice, and simple daily conversations. During winter vacation, we use free English listening apps and choose audio from cartoons the child likes to “soak his ears.” Even though there is screen time each day, listening is more important than watching and is more effective for improving English.

Math is relatively abstract, and many parents easily fall into the trap of “doing endless exercises.” A better approach is to rely on the child’s curiosity and build interest in math through daily life—counting pocket money, counting floors in an elevator, calculating points while playing cards, guessing heads or tails of coins, and so on. Turn abstract concepts into visible everyday things, and gradually build mathematical thinking.

This is how our family builds a learning environment. It doesn’t require expensive spending. Everyone can do it, and it may be the simplest—and also the most effective—way of learning.
