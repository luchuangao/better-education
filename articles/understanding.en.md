# Understanding

In daily life, it’s inevitable that children and adults will run into all kinds of conflicts—big or small. Sometimes a very tiny thing can trigger a disagreement, and the root cause is often simple: we don’t understand the same thing in the same way. A gap forms between your understanding and your child’s understanding.

Einstein once said, “If you can’t explain it simply, you don’t understand it well enough.” Many people think teaching children is the teacher’s job, or that teaching children is easy. But teaching itself is not simple.

Only when you can truly teach a child—and the child can, in turn, teach you—does it count as real understanding. That’s why many questions from children may look simple, yet we don’t know how to answer them. Often, it’s because we don’t genuinely understand the question ourselves.

As for what “understanding” really means, I resonate with Richard Feynman’s definition:

> “What I cannot create, I do not understand.”

He also said:

> “If you want to master something, teach it.”

Questions that seem “stupid” can be worth serious attention. Behind them is often a need for deeper learning. When you help a child understand something, you’re also re-learning it yourself.

Every time my son asks me a question, I try to answer seriously. I’ve realized that for many questions, I truly don’t know how to answer. So I learn first—through search engines or AI—and then explain it to him. After he understands, he usually repeats his own understanding back to me. Many children naturally know how to learn by teaching others, and that repetition is learning through teaching.

So here’s the most practical question: do you have the patience to answer every doubt? If you really don’t know how to answer, it means you don’t know either. Admit it honestly—say you don’t know or don’t understand yet—and learn together with your child. That is often the best way to get along, and the best way to resolve conflicts.
