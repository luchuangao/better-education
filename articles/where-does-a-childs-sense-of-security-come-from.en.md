# Where Does a Child's Sense of Security Come From?

Where does a child's sense of security come from? In the last article, I talked about the problem of children "not listening", and the time he shows his autonomy most often is when playing outdoors.

Today I want to continue this topic and talk about my understanding of "sense of security".

When I was a child, my parents and neighbors always exhorted in my ears: "Don't play outside alone" "Don't go out at night, there are people stealing children". This fear of being stolen, like a faint shadow, shrouded my entire childhood. But it never really stopped me from going out. As long as I played with my friends, all fears were thrown to the back of my mind. It was only when I was tired of playing and wanted to go home that the trace of fear loomed again, and then I ran back in a hurry. Nowadays, I encourage my child to play outdoors more. I also try to accompany him as much as possible. This is not because there are fewer bad people now, but because children should not be locked at home like pets every day, should not be asked to sit quietly at the desk, and even more should not be dismissed by a mobile phone.

I believe that the vast majority of a child's sense of security and self-confidence actually comes from athletic ability. Life establishes control over one's own body in exploration and recognizes the scale of risk in attempts. Children will of course do dangerous actions; they are constantly testing the boundaries of safety. For example, my son playing on the slide, starting from more than three years old, chasing, climbing over railings, sliding backwards... In the eyes of adults, these are not standard enough, and even a bit dangerous. But I rarely stop him directly, and observe more on the side how he faces fear step by step and adjusts himself. He has certainly fallen, bumped, and cried, but slowly, he has achieved movements that many children cannot do, as smooth as playing parkour. After successfully completing a high-difficulty movement every time, his complacent look is his happiest moment. Playing until he doesn't want to go home is a common occurrence.

My character was weak when I was a child, often bullied, and had experience of being bullied at school. At that time, I didn't like outdoor sports. When I was beaten, I often just froze in place. So, now I pay great attention to cultivating my child's physical ability and reaction awareness. He has not been bad at sports since he was a child. Running and skipping rope are among the best in the class. His posture in playing basketball and badminton is better than mine. I encourage him: If someone hits you, you must protect yourself, you can hit back, but never start it, just defense, never intentionally hurt people. If the other party is relentless, stop immediately and tell the teacher or parents. I always believe that a child's greatest sense of security comes from "I can protect myself", rather than just relying on external forces. Of course, I have also heard children playing together say that he has "heavy hands", so I will repeatedly remind him: You can protect yourself, but don't let others get hurt. He also once injured others due to excessive defense. We came to apologize, but the premise is that the other party started it first.

> Psychologist Jordan Peterson once said that competence is what makes a person feel safe to the greatest extent.

What I can teach my child is actually very simple: learn to be independent from an early age, learn to face problems with your own strength and way. Although he is only 6 years old now, the road ahead is long. What he can rely on in the end is only himself, a strong body, a flexible mind, and an independent heart.
