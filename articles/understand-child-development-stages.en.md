# Understand Child Development Stages

It is important to understand the stages of child development. During the summer vacation last year, I really paid attention to this issue because I found that my son always likes to understand the things he knows by personification. Through learning, I realized Jean Piaget's cognitive development theory and realized that he is in the pre-operational stage of 2-7 years old. Children at this stage usually show the characteristics of self-centeredness, thinking easily dominated by emotions, and relatively weak abstract logic ability.

Before, my understanding of his behavior was completely vague. After realizing this, I adjusted the way I get along with him, mainly making the following adjustments:

**First, talk less reason and tell more stories**
Using vivid and interesting stories, especially through anthropomorphic animals or plants to convey truths, is easier for children to accept. For example, comparing developing good habits to a tree, only by frequent pruning can it grow tall and straight, instead of becoming a crooked neck tree.

**Accept emotions and guide in time**
At this stage, emotions come and go quickly, so there is no need to rush to deny them. First allow him to have emotions, sometimes you can temporarily avoid them, let the emotions ease naturally, and then help him analyze together afterwards, guiding him to recognize his current feelings.

**Provide choices and cultivate a sense of control**
Give more multiple-choice questions instead of commands. For example, ask him if he wants to play for a while or read a book for a while. He has slowly formed his own rhythm in life, such as liking to read after exercise or before going to bed, and liking to play drums before meals. Such choices can help him gradually establish a sense of autonomy and control.

**Enlighten mathematics in concrete ways**
Children in the pre-operational stage may have good language skills, but their mathematical abstraction ability is still relatively weak. Generally, they can truly understand until they enter the concrete operational stage at the age of 7. Now we can use visible and tangible ways such as counting fingers and managing pocket money to help him gradually establish a number sense in life. The goal is not to calculate quickly and well, but not to hate mathematics.

**Value companionship, especially playing**
Try to take an hour every day to play, learn, read, and exchange ideas with him wholeheartedly. Many people tend to only accompany learning but not playing, but in fact, for children at this stage, playing is more important than learning. It is precisely because the child is still in the cognitive stage of "self-centered, emotion-dominated, and weak abstract logic" that games and interactions in companionship are the most important ways for him to learn and grow.

Only by understanding and adapting to the child's development stage, perhaps we can avoid some detours and avoid becoming an unqualified parent.
