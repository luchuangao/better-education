# The Best Education

What is the best education? I don't consider myself a qualified father yet; I still have a lot to learn. But in my heart, our child is a qualified child, happy every day, completely the "child from another family" in others' eyes. Although he occasionally annoys me, most of the time, he brings me full happiness and childhood-like healing.

Today, as a self-proclaimed "unqualified" father, I want to share some of my educational insights.

## First, I won't let my child do things I hated as a child.

I was not good at studying when I was young, and my memory was poor. It took me a long time to memorize a text, and I would forget it soon after, easily falling into a vicious cycle. Only after growing up did I understand that it was because there were too few books to read at that time; textbooks were almost the only world, and I lived like a frog in a well.

Therefore, I rarely force my child to do homework or memorize texts, but try to read with him as much as possible. I believe reading and painting are more important than mechanical homework. From prenatal education, I have told him stories every day, almost without interruption. In the last two years, reading has become a part of his life—even if I don't have time to accompany him, he will pick up a book and read it himself. As for school homework, he can complete it relatively quickly, and he memorizes texts and ancient poems easily. I think this is probably the subtle influence of reading more books.

## Second, I have the shadow of domestic violence, but I don't hit or scold my child.

When I was young, I was often chased and beaten by my father; being whipped with a belt was a common occurrence. Because I hated school, the principal even said, "If you don't beat him for three days, he won't study." I was once a passive learner who was subjected to violence. Do you say I don't have violent tendencies? Actually, I do. But when I was young, I was powerless to resist. After growing up, I chose to reconcile with the past and try not to think back.

When my son was three years old, I spanked him once, and since then I have hardly ever hit him again, and rarely scolded him. I never force him to study because I know passive learning is meaningless. Everyone has the right to choose their own path and should not live entirely according to the wishes of others.

## Third, I was rarely encouraged, but I often encourage my child.

Since I was a child, I was not a very popular child and rarely received affirmation. But for my son, I often have words of encouragement on my lips. Even if he doesn't do well or doesn't do well in exams, as long as he tries his best, I will encourage him. Of course, there is criticism, but very little, such as occasionally playing games overtime. It is precisely because there is more encouragement that he will do better and better; and it is also because encouragement is the norm that occasional criticism will be truly listened to.

---

> Socrates once said: "The unexamined life is not worth living."

Now, I treat my child's education like treating my former self. Making up for his regrets, guarding his freedom, and witnessing his blooming. Perhaps this is the best education I can give my child—not a perfect template, but love and companionship with awareness.

In our family, my child and I are more like partners. I always believe that **behind every child who loves learning, there are often parents who love learning. Only when parents and children grow together is it the best education.**
