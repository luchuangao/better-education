# Give Children Space

True independence begins with a sense of control over independent space.

When I was young, there were many brothers and sisters in my family. We slept in one bed, had no room of our own, and the place was stuffed with things everywhere, with absolutely no personal space. My older sister or younger brother often stayed at relatives' houses, and that period made me feel particularly at ease. Most children nowadays have their own rooms from an early age. My son took the initiative to ask to sleep in his own bed when he was four years old. After sleeping alone for a period of time, I could feel that he had obvious independent consciousness, and his emotions became slightly more stable. Later, he got sick once and didn't want to sleep alone anymore. Although we suggested it many times, he refused. It wasn't until we agreed that he must sleep alone after his sixth birthday that he truly adapted after his birthday. Sometimes children need a push to develop independent habits as early as possible, because a clear sense of boundaries is often built on autonomous control of space.

In addition to sleeping, he also has his own bookcase, shoe cabinet, toy cabinet and snack cabinet, which are all his territory. Without his permission, we will not take his things or eat his snacks without authorization, nor will we enter his room at will. When he is concentrating on playing with toys or reading books, try not to disturb him and give him space to be fully involved.

True independence also begins with having privacy. Unless he shares it voluntarily, we will not ask about his privacy. His phone watch password and payment password are set by himself, and red envelopes given by others are also transferred to his Alipay account. He rarely spends money recklessly. Compared with many children who love to buy snacks, he appears more restrained. Occasionally we sell toys in the park together, and the money earned is divided equally, letting him experience from an early age that making money requires effort, and he will also sell hard.

When he was four years old, he had a period of mood swings and tantrums. In the past two years, when he is emotional, he will hide in his room and be quiet for a while, and he will slowly get better. We were worried at first, but later gradually understood that this is his way of dealing with emotions. Many times he loses his temper because previous rules were broken, such as playing all day and having no time to read with him at night. When I deal with such conflicts, I usually lift them high and put them down gently. As long as he realizes the problem, I will still accompany him to read for a while.

Growth begins with independence, and independence begins with a space of one's own. Independence is originally the child's inner drive. As an unqualified father, I remind myself that the last thing I should do is to deprive him of the space he needs for growth.
