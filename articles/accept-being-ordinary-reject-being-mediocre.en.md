# Accept Being Ordinary, Reject Being Mediocre

I accept that my child is an ordinary child because I am also an ordinary person. But at the same time, I don't completely hope that he is limited by the word ordinary. I don't want him to live only by secular standards, but look forward to him finding his own passion. He doesn't need to pursue great wealth and honor, as long as he can take good care of himself and live truly and freely.

As social animals, we are inevitably bound by the eyes and rules of the outside world, and even hide our true thoughts and restrain our true selves for this reason. Because of this, I hope to help him get close to his inner voice from an early age. I encourage him to try new things, not be afraid of others' evaluations, and not be limited by too many frameworks. Over the years, he has been exposed to painting, chess, skating, taekwondo, drums, basketball, rock climbing... Some he didn't like at first, but gradually became interested in the process; some he did easily and better than other children. It is difficult to determine which are his truly lasting passions now, but what matters is that he is getting to know himself in the attempt.

In reading, we also set no limits. Even if there are many books that seem to be beyond his age understanding, he will look at the pictures and gradually understand part of the content. When playing, I rarely remind him of danger constantly like other parents, saying you can't do this or that, but tell him: when you feel a little fear, be careful. I try to give only basic principles, such as crossing the road must wait for the green light, do not run, rather than directing every movement in detail. I hope he learns to feel risks and judge independently, rather than just obeying instructions.

> Luo Xiang said that what is more terrible than poverty is living a life without expectation every day, that is just living.

I agree deeply. For my child, my biggest wish is that he can maintain expectations in the ordinary and light up his own glimmer in the ordinary.

Of course, if one day he says he wants to be an ordinary person like me, we will also accept it happily, as long as that is the life he truly identifies with. Everyone is ultimately responsible only for themselves. I have had stages that were not satisfactory or even very unhappy, but that is just my personal experience; everyone's mentality and feelings can be different in the face of similar experiences. And what I can do is accompany him to experience, to choose, and then respect him to become what he wants to become.
