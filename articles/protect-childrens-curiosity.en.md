# Protect Children's Curiosity

The essence of education lies in awakening and empowerment, and the core is to let children recognize and realize their own potential. Among them, protecting children's curiosity and ability to explore independently is crucial. Many people observe that when children reach the second or third grade of elementary school, the light in their eyes disappears, and they become dull and lack vitality. The root cause is often the result of curiosity being bound or even strangled. Once curiosity about the world is lost, children will naturally lose vitality, be indifferent to new things, and even start to escape.

As parents, we should consciously protect children's curiosity from the beginning, which requires three points: recognize the drawbacks of traditional education, be aware of one's own fixed mindset, and be brave to make changes.

**First, recognize the drawbacks of traditional education.**
Traditional education often pursues certainty and material success. Its typical characteristics are taking scores as the yardstick and obedience as the criterion. Teachers who focus more on scores are sometimes more popular with parents, but more resented by children. We must clearly recognize that the subject of education is the child, not the parents and teachers. Many current practices are essentially putting the cart before the horse.

**Second, be aware of one's own fixed mindset.**
Many parents find it difficult to detect their own problems. On the one hand, most people have never personally experienced other educational models; on the other hand, regardless of whether parents themselves are beneficiaries of exam-oriented education, they may fall into the cycle of involution of children. Beneficiaries hope to replicate success, and victims hope to make up for regrets. However, the direct responsible persons for children's growth are parents. Even if a small number of parents try to change, most of the makers of the education system are still beneficiaries of the traditional model, and weak voices are difficult to promote systemic changes.

**Third, the key lies in making practical changes.**
Specifically, start by reducing denial of children. You might as well count how many times you say "no" or "can't" to your child in a day. If you are not sure what is the absolutely correct way, parents might as well reduce arbitrary interference and allow children to explore freely. What's the harm in delaying homework time a little? Homework itself cannot truly endow children with the core ability to cope with the future.

If you cannot completely give up homework for the time being, you can try to only remind and return the right of choice to the child: it's time for homework, you decide whether to write it now or not. Even if he doesn't write it, the child will not lose truly important things, because key abilities are by no means obtained through mechanical homework or doing questions. Take English learning as an example. From elementary school to university, how many people can truly communicate fluently? Is this ability practiced by doing homework and test papers? Since we know that this method is inefficient, still forcing children to execute it is often just to appease parents' own anxiety, which will only make children as the subject of learning more and more resistant to learning.

Making changes starts with respecting children's curiosity and right of choice, and returning the main status of exploration and growth to them.
