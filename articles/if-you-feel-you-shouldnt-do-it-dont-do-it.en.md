# If You Feel You Shouldn't Do It, Don't Do It

Today I took my child to play in the garden downstairs. After eating hawthorn candy, my son asked me, "Can I throw the candy wrapper in the garden?" I didn't answer directly, but said to him: **Before doing anything, if you feel you shouldn't do it, don't do it.** In fact, when asking, he already vaguely knew the answer in his heart. I chose not to tell him directly, but to guide him to think about whether he should do this, hoping to cultivate his habit of independent judgment. In the end, he didn't throw it away casually, but gave the candy wrapper to me and turned away to play.

Before doing anything, if you feel you shouldn't do it, don't do it. This sentence reminds me of what my idol Naval said, "If you can't decide, the answer is no," and Duan Yongping's "Don't touch if you don't understand." Essentially, they are all saying: trust your first instinct. For children, it is normal to have doubts or make mistakes. Although he has learned not to litter since kindergarten, he still throws it away occasionally. I think this is mainly influenced by other children, or because of momentary laziness. Playing with children who don't pay much attention to hygiene, he may feel that it doesn't matter if he throws it away, but that moment of hesitation actually tells him: this is wrong.

Everyone knows the story of "Mencius' Mother Moving Three Times". The environment does deeply affect people. But changing the environment often costs a lot. What we can do is to manage ourselves and our children first. Sometimes when my son sees garbage on the ground, he will also take the initiative to pick it up and throw it into the trash can. I always believe: Environment > Example > Preaching. If the environment cannot be changed temporarily, start by changing yourself.

Why should you trust your intuition? Because intuition is a comprehensive reflection of a person's entire experience, it contains what things cannot be done and what is beneficial to oneself. Children make more mistakes when they are young, so they can avoid detours when they grow up. I won't follow him and say "you can't do this, you can't touch that" endlessly, only teaching him the most basic principles. If everything is blocked, he will not know how to act. If he throws garbage casually, I will tell him that this may lead to more people littering. Usually he will pick it up. If he doesn't pick it up, I will pick it up myself and won't say more. Because action is more powerful than language, that is, example is greater than preaching, even if he doesn't understand now, he will slowly understand in the future.

If intuition is regarded as an algorithm, it may be closer to Bayes' theorem, constantly updating one's cognition and judgment through continuous experience. Experiencing more mistakes from an early age is actually helpful for improving judgment. But not all mistakes can be made: life safety, law, morality, and integrity are the bottom lines that must be kept. Because life is only once, it is not a bet on the poker table, and it is worth our constant caution.
