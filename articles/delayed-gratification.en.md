# Delayed Gratification

The famous Stanford marshmallow experiment revealed an interesting phenomenon: most children eat the snack immediately after receiving it, while only a small number can resist temptation and save it for later. Research suggests that children who can wait often become more successful in the future, because they have stronger self-control.

My son has been the “willing to wait” type since he was little. At first I thought it was due to a sense of possession—worrying that once he ate it, it would be gone. But even when I told him there would be more later, most of the time he still chose to save it, and when he ate it he often took the initiative to share. You could say delayed gratification seems like an inborn trait for him. He is steady, doesn’t like to compete for things, works slowly but steadily, and his emotions have remained relatively calm.

This trait also shows up in many aspects of his growth: in sports, he won first place in jump-rope competitions in kindergarten and elementary school; in drumming, he is among the best of his peers; academically, he has consistently done well and we hardly need to push him to do homework. Overall, he is a child who gives parents little worry.

From a broader perspective, delayed gratification is often closely linked to long-term thinking, optimism, and a compounding mindset. Having the patience to persist every day, believing the future will be better, and trusting that accumulation can lead to qualitative change—these motivations may all come from the ability to “delay rewards and wait for greater returns.”

So should parents be proud to have such a child? In my view, what matters more than innate personality is the sense of security and trust provided by the family. All of this is demonstrated by parents through daily words and actions. Children are observers and imitators. Therefore, becoming someone worth imitating is the most important responsibility of being a parent. At the same time, creating an environment where children dare to compete, dare to take risks, and build resilience is even more important than temperament itself.
